Marie Curie

Childhood

Maria Sklodowska, or Manya as was her nickname, learned to be a Polish patriot from her parents, Bronislawa and Vladislav Sklodowski. In 1867, Poland was still under Russian rule and had been sliced up among Austria, Prussia, and Russia. The Russian czar aimed to eliminate Polish culture as one would stamp on a cockroach. But the Polish grimly held on to their nationality and identity. Maria's parents were teachers as well, and so took the opportunity to undermine Russian oppression by fueling their children with Polish culture and scientific knowledge.

When Maria was born her mother was forced to quit her position as school head to support the family. The children were then taught at a boys' high school where their father taught mathematics and physics for a good pay, but he was fired for his Polish advocacy. Marie Curie said, "Constantly held in suspicion and spied upon, the children knew that a single conversation in Polish, or an imprudent word, might seriously harm, not only themselves, but also their families."

To support themselves as a family, they were forced to accept student boarders. But Maria's older sister Zosia caught typhus and died, soon followed by her mother. Now there was just the five of them - Vladislav, Hela, Maria, Jozef, and Bronya.
