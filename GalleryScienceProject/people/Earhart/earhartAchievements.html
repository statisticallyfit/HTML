Amelia Earhart

Achievements


Amelia Earhart accomplished much in her 40 years.

She took her first flying lesson with pilot Neta Snook on January 3, 1921 and worked odd jobs to finance these lessons. Six months later, she bought her first yellow Kinner Airster biplane and named it the Canary. Receiving her flying license from the National Aeronautic Association, she flew in the Pacific Coast Ladies' Derby in Pasadena merely two days later. A year later after receiving an international license from a French organization, she took a three year break from aviation upon hearing of her parents' divorce, and so re-enrolled in university.

Fortunately, in 1927 she rejoined aviation. She and pilot Wilmer Stultz and co-pilot Louis Gordon traveled from Newfoundland to Wales in a twenty-hour flight aboard the Friendship. Despite the growing fame of being the first female passenger on a trans-Atlantic flight, Amelia remained humble. She noted: "Stultz did all the flying - had to. Intent on fulfilling her goal, Amelia bought a Lockheed Vega in August 1929 and competed in a derby race from Santa Monica to Cleveland. She won third place. In the months that followed, she set numerous speed and distance records.

True to her word, Amelia became the first woman to fly solo over the Atlantic from May 21 to May 20 in 1932. This gained her the "Distinguished Flying Cross from Congress, the Cross of Knight of the Legion of Honor from the French government, and the Gold Medal of the National Geographic Society from President Hoover." Three months later, Amelia also covered the North American continent. In the following two years, she continued setting records and gaining resources for her aviation dream.

Later in 1937, Amelia Earhart completed a trans-continental flight from Oakland, California to Honolulu, Hawaii. President Franklin D. Roosevelt praised her in a letter because she had showed the " 'doubting Thomases' that aviation is a science which cannot be limited to men only."

But June 1, 1937 changed all that. Amelia and her navigator Fred Noonan left from Miami, Florida with the goal of reaching Howland Island in the Pacific, in a Pacific route. Intrigued, they set off. They completed 22,000 miles or two-thirds of their flight, touching on South America, Africa, India, and Lae, New Guinea. But once they departed from New Guniea, horror struck them - they could not find Howland Island. Worse, they lost radio contact with the coast guard and disappeared somewhere over the Pacific Ocean.
