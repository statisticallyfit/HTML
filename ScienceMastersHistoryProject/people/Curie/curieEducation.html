Despite the family's financial suffering, the "children honored [their father] for nurturing them emotionally and intellectually." On Saturday nights, he would read classic literature to them. Moreover, he showed them scientific equipment he now kept at home due to Russian regulations. Because of their father's care towards their well-being, the children grew to reach intellectual heights. Easily and passionately absorbing their father's teaching, Maria said she enjoyed her father's help but was sad that he had no laboratory or equipment to teach them experimentation. Many times the top student in her class as a child, Maria was awarded the Nobel Prize twice, Hela became a teacher, and Jozef and Bronya became physicians. Maria noted, "We all had much facility for intellectual work."

As two young women, Maria and Bronya had few financial resources due to their mother's death and their father's financial losses. But they bravely made a pact: Maria would work to raise money for Bronya's education, and then Bronya would later do the same. This way, both would get their education.

And so, for the next few years, Maria worked as a tutor and governess, and in the evenings, if she had time, she would read chemistry, physics and mathematics books. Also, she would study science and Polish culture at night at an illegal school for Poles.

In Paris in November 1891, when Maria was 24 years old, she studied physics, chemistry, and mathematics at the prestigious Sorbonne University. This was a hard time for Maria; the winters in her unheated apartment had the effect of freezing her to the bone.
